Common Negative Thought Patterns

  • "Shoulds": Thoughts about how you or other people "should" be can lead to guilt, anger, or shame. They also don't help you think about how to fix a problem.
    • Example: "I should be better at..."
  • Dwelling on the negative/discounting the positive: you single out only the negative details, or discount the positive, so your reality becomes darkened.
    • Example: If someone rejects you, you think to yourself, "Nobody likes me."
    • Example: If someone compliments you, you think, "She's just saying that to be nice."
  • Catastrophizing: Exaggerating the importance of problems.
    • Example: "My boss got mad at me - he's going fire me."
    • Example: "If I split up with my partner, I'll never meet anyone again, and will be alone."
  • Overgeneralizing: Seeing a single negative event as a never ending pattern (often using words like "always" or "never").
    • Example: After an awkward social moment, you think, "I never know what to say to people."
  • Magical Thinking: You make assumptions about what others are thinking (like a mind reader) or about what the future holds (like a psychic).
    • Example: "I know exactly what she's thinking."
    • Example: "If I say that, I know he'll get mad."
    • Example: "He did that on purpose."

Some of these negative thought patterns overlap. As you begin to pay attention to negative automatic thoughts, the goal is to see which patterns fit for you.
